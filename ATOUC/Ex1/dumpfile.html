My name is Dr. April Boudreau and the below is my best recollection of the events that occured in October of 1997. In the early 90s, I owned a small private forensics lab which I’d founded to capture overflow from criminal pathology labs and provide services to the private market. The latter turned out to be more consistent and profitable as the shortfall from the government labs was mostly caused by budget constraints.

We’d been hired to help a consortium of airlines investigate deaths on their flights they feared were caused by Deep Vein Thrombosis. DVT is the formation of a blood clot in a deep vein, most commonly the legs.  About half of cases have no symptoms, but complications may include a fatal pulmonary embolism when the clot detaches and travels to the lungs.

There was a lot of media hysteria at the time, and they felt a lawsuit was inevitable, so they were keen to get ahead of the problem by researching their defence.

In each case, we’d autopsy the body and confirm that the cause of death. The families of the deceased agreed to it as the airlines were offering to cover all funeral expenses and whatever other inducements were required to convince them to cooperate.

One such case though was an exception. The man in question had died without a will or any lawful heir. What complicated matters was he had in the year before his death got back in touch with a daughter he wasn’t aware he’d had. When her mother had died the year before she’d found his contact details and reconnected.

The lack of heirs presented a problem for the airline, so with the daughters permission they requested a court injunction to first prove paternity and then perform the autopsy. If the test came up false, the body would be returned to the state, so there was an obvious incentive for the airline to prove parentage quickly.

We received the corpse on the 2nd of October 1997, nine days after his death during a Mid-Atlantic flight from Sydney to LA. It was in remarkably good condition even considering the fact it had been kept refrigerated for the duration.

Problems occurred almost immediately. The paternity tests even in 1997 were a fairly routine affair and running one on a corpse was something our lab had successfully done already on two occasions. Each time the test would simply fail to run; we couldn’t get it to return any meaningful result. Eventually, we started to examine the samples, and that’s when the problems started.

My initial hypothesis was that some contaminant was disrupting the DNA profiling process needed to run a paternity test. I decided to run a karyotype which is simply a picture of someone’s chromosomes from their white blood cells. You may have seen it in highschool textbooks, 22 X shaped blots that contain all our DNA plus 1 more that determines gender.

Instead what I saw was bizarre. No blurry X shapes but rather a single six armed structure meeting in the centre like an asterisk. I wasn’t concerned by the first result, but as the second, third, and fourth tests all came back the same, all from separate samples, I realised I was facing something both beyond my abilities to understand on my own and yet too strange to show anyone else.

By this point, we were under serious pressure from the airline to give them the paternity test they were after, and it shames me to say it, but I faked the results. I’m not proud of what I did, and I’m not defending my actions, but with no other heirs, I simply couldn’t see the harm. I’d also have to admit that part of me wanted to start the autopsy and find a solution to this puzzle.

We quickly confirmed the cause of death; Pulmonary embolism from a blood clot that formed in his leg mid-flight. With that confirmed, we collected the data the airlines had contracted us to: body weight, cholesterol levels, signs of heart disease, etc. Then I returned to the problem of the paternity test, starting with the leg bone exposed during the autopsy.

I extracted a portion of the bone marrow on the hypothesis that it might be spared from what I still thought was some unknown contamination, but it came back with the exact same result. A single chromosome with six arms all terminating at a central point, a configuration that doesn’t exist anywhere in nature.

From a forensic standpoint everything felt off, even the rate of decomposition was unusual. Deciding I needed to change my approach  I put a bone sample under a microscope. Healthy bone looks smooth to the eye and touch, but in reality, under a microscope, it’s a rough honeycomb structure that makes the bone light yet strong.

This bone had that same structure but perfectly formed and regular. Even healthy bone is full of small imperfections and irregularities. This one was like something extruded or grown in a lab; I half expected to see a medical implant part number. Importantly, it also lacked any sign of functioning Osteoblast and Osteoclasts. These cells constantly break down and rebuild the bone, but none were present.

 I didn’t know what to do with what I’d learnt, and reaching out for help would come with a level of professional scrutiny that I wasn’t prepared for. Especially when it would reveal the fact that I’d faked the paternity test. Left with no other options, I reached out to the airline and told them there were problems with the autopsy.

I never told them exactly what I’d seen, only using vague references to ‘anomalous results’ and ‘inconclusive evidence’. They didn’t seem in the least bit phased. Apparently, they’d decided that the lawyers had enough physical evidence and now the defence strategy had shifted. I wasn’t aware of it, but as part of the DVT investigation they’d also started recording the flights and invited me to review them for the first time. I almost stopped them to explain my background in forensics wouldn’t be much help but curiosity got the better of me, and I said yes.
