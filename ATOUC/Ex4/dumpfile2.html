I arrived late on the 12th Oct 1997 to review the recordings. They made no effort to hide the fact that this was purely about avoiding litigation. I quickly realised that I was the only one with any medical background as everyone else was either from their legal department or middle management.

The Airline Group was paying by the hour so I was content to sit through the tapes. I was only interested in learning more about the mysterious corpse still in the morgue at my lab. Each of the recordings was incredibly monotonous. They all followed the same pattern of plane takeoff, dinner, movie, sleep, breakfast and then landing.

By the third day we got to the video I’d been waiting for. By this point everyone had stopped coming to the meetings except for the project manager, an intern, and an elderly lawyer from the legal department. Only the intern and I were paying any attention to the videos. The project manager kept himself busy with a bulky mid-90s laptop and the lawyer simply read his newspaper and dozed.

We’d gotten ourselves into a pattern that roughly followed the routine of the passengers. Spend the morning watching the takeoff, in-flight dinner and movie, and then had lunch while the passengers slept. I was distracted with my lunch, the lawyer with his crossword, so it was the intern who first asked “What’s he doing?” between mouthfuls.

The screen was divided into four views: one for the pilots, economy cabin, business class, and the single galley section between them. The intern was motioning to the bottom right with his sandwich. This view covered the galley section. The screen was grainy but you could clearly see one of the flight attendants. He had arranged small disposable cups on a tray to be filled with water. Instead, he just kept rearranging them, lining them up, pausing, and then shuffling them around only to begin again. We watched him repeat this continuously for five minutes before very calmly sitting down on the floor and then after a moment lying on his side.

The four of us simply watched this in silence. There was no indication that anyone else in the airplane had seen this or was reacting; it appeared that everyone else was asleep. Suddenly the silence was broken by a shout from the intern. He dived across the boardroom table and scrambled for the remote to pause the video.

“Look,” he said, gesturing at the screen.
	“We know, he’s lying down.”
“No, no, look!” he said, pointing wildly at the screen.
	The project manager finally spoke up. “Buddy, you’re going to have to be more specific.”
“It’s open…”
	“What is?”
“The door, the cabin door.”
We stared at the top left screen. It was just a silhouette in the dark at the far end of the aisle but it was clear that the door to the plane was open.

“That’s not possible,” the lawyer quietly commented.
	“No fucking shit, nobody is allowed to open the door mid-flight,” the project manager snapped.
“No son, it’s physically not possible. The door swings inward by design so that it can only be opened if the air pressure outside is the same.”
	“Hit play,” commanded the project manager.
The intern pressed play and we hunched over the boardroom table with a focus we’d never found in the past three days. In one corner was the flight attendant still curled up on his side, above that was the silhouette of the open door in the economy cabin, and the other two screens showed everyone either asleep or quietly repeating the same actions.

“Is it playing?” the project manager asked.
	“Yeah,” the intern muttered.
“Are you sure? Because it still looks paused.”
	“It’s playing, it’s just that nothing is moving. You can see the light blinking in the -”
The color drained from his face as he slumped in his chair. On the screen something half-seen was coming through the door, with long articulated legs like some deep sea crab. Each carefully reaching into the plane until connecting with a wall, chair or floor. Nobody reacted, there were no screams or shouts of surprise as it squeezed into the plane.

The intern turned and vomited violently into a nearby waste bin. The project manager stared at his knuckles gripping the table while the lawyer and I remained transfixed.
Eventually the legs had found purchase and slowly a large central mass where they converged pulled itself into the cabin. The legs picked their way through the cabin, each movement bringing the central hub further into the plane until eventually it was suspended above the middle of the cabin. We could now see that some dark lump was slung beneath and a smaller set of appendages was unravelling it.

The lawyer motioned to this as something was plucked loose and then in a single movement the passenger below was lifted up and swiftly replaced by whatever had been carried in.
Doubling checking the seating chart confirmed my suspicion. “That’s our victim, that’s where he was sitting.”
I then realised that whatever he was replaced with was still sitting in the morgue back at my lab.

“Guess that rules out DVT then,” the lawyer quietly remarked.
	The project manager stood and made for the door.
“Hold on,” I said, “Let’s take a moment before we talk to others about -”
	“I’m not telling anyone, you’re not telling anyone!” the project manager snapped, “That’s my fucking flight, I take that flight, I take that flight every month, I’ve had this -”
“There is no reason to think that you -”
	“I have every reason!” the project manager shouted as he fumbled with the door and stormed out of the office.
“Should we stop him?” I asked distractedly.
	The lawyer looked out the conference room and then came back in, “No, it looks like he’s just going back to his office on this floor. Suspect he probably has a bottle of scotch tucked away.”
“You seem calm.”
	“I’ve lived a storied life; was in the military before passing the bar. What about yourself?”
“I had forewarning; I’d already autopsied whatever it was that they just replaced that man with.”

The legs were moving back towards the door and then it left just as carefully as it entered. Slowly, the door swung shut, and after a brief moment of what looked like turbulence the plane was still. Ten minutes later the flight attendant stood back up and resumed his shuffling of the cups only to suddenly start filling them with water as if nothing had happened. He then handed the cups out to passengers and everyone seemed to return to normal. Fast forwarding through we saw the breakfast service begin and it was only at that point a flight attendant found that the victim had “died”.

I turned to the intern who was still sitting hunched in the corner looking pale. “Are you okay?”
	“Yes, I’m just, I -” He jumped suddenly as a gunshot cracked from down the hall.
The lawyer and I ran toward the noise but we knew what we’d find before we got to the project manager’s office. He’d wheeled a TV and VCR stand into his office from a nearby meeting room. On his desk his calendar was open to a date three months ago which listed a flight to Sydney. The video running on the TV was from a different flight but was the same scene we’d witnessed before, paused just as the first leg was entering the cabin.

We rang the police and explained what had happened, minus the details of the tape. While we waited for them to arrive we fast forwarded through the second tape. The thing entered the cabin only briefly and then left without touching anyone.
	“I’m taking this,” I said, motioning to the second tape.
“Taking what?” said the lawyer, leaving the office.

When the police arrived they quickly decided it was just another overworked office worker committing suicide. We were offered counseling and sent home. The next day a contingent from the government arrived. I can only assume the lawyer had been in touch with them. They were unwilling to discuss the details; they simply served me a legal notice that explained that what I’d seen was now classified and made clear the repercussions of sharing it or making it public.

After that, I never had any contact with any of them. I googled both the lawyer and the intern over the years. The lawyer died of what looked like natural causes in 2011 and the intern basically dropped off the face of the earth. I’ve never managed to find him again.
